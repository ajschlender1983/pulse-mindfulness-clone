Do's, don'ts & usage rules

The whole grammar in one glance — what every frame must do, what gets it archived on sight, and the four rules the workshop runs on. The summary at the bottom stays live as you rate frames red below.

Do

  • Soft ambient natural light, warm cream / honey palette, medium-format film feel.
  • The wide polished gold ring as a discovered second-glance glint — present, never a hard sell.
  • Real, diverse people in true moments — editorial, un-staged, emotionally honest.
  • Two visual worlds kept distinct: warm Maya lifestyle vs. dark luxe product.
  • Anti-data everywhere: no screens-of-metrics, no streaks, nothing to check.
  • Motion earns its place — the pulse is always the hinge that slows the moment.

Don't

  • Malformed hands — extra/fused/six fingers, bent joints, melted hands. Archive on sight.
  • Uncanny/waxy faces, broken eyes or teeth, warped anatomy, extra limbs.
  • Text, watermarks, gibberish signage, or brand logos in the frame.
  • Cold, stocky, corporate, or over-saturated CGI looks. No glow on cream.
  • The ring rendered wrong — a thin band, a diamond ring, doubled, or mangled.
  • Hype grammar (urgency, scarcity, "drop", exclamation points).

Usage rules

  1. Cream editorial for everything felt; dark luxe for the product / logistics. Arrival day is the deliberate crossover.
  2. Hands are the #1 slop risk — every visible hand gets a finger-count check before a frame ships.
  3. Rate honestly: green = ship, yellow = usable with a caveat, red = archive + note why.
  4. Every red note names the failure (hands / light / anatomy / off-brand / ring) so it can adjust a prompt.

Filmmaking

The filmmaking lexicon & motion rules

Pulse borrows the grammar of film because the product is a film idea: your life is twenty-four frames a second, and the only question is whether you're watching it at the speed you can live. Here's the vocabulary, where each term is used, and the motion rules — including the specific cinematic law for illustration, for photograph, and for the moment in between.

Frame the unit
A single still instant. Twenty-four of them a second make a life.
In Pulse — every emailed moment ("the kettle," "the last page") is one frame asked to be exposed long enough to register.
Frame rate the speed
Frames per second. Too fast and it blurs; held longer and it becomes memory.
In Pulse — autopilot is 60 frames a minute, none seen; presence is 24 frames of one moment. "A pulse is a frame rate; presence is choosing the rate."
Aperture the iris
The iris that opens to let light in and set what's sharp.
In Pulse — attention is the aperture. The pulse opens it; the room floods back in.
Focus / rack focus the pull
The lens resolving a subject from blur to sharp; racking shifts focus between planes.
In Pulse — the ring racks focus off the future (blurred) and onto the now (sharp). This is the transition motion rule.
Lens the choice
What the camera sees through. Choosing the lens is choosing attention.
In Pulse — "Not more footage. The same footage, at the speed of being here." Presence is choosing the lens, not adding film.
Depth of field what's sharp
How much is in focus. Shallow = one plane sharp, the rest soft.
In Pulse — presence is shallow depth of field on the moment you're standing in; everything ahead of it goes soft.
The hold the dwell
Letting a shot run longer than expected until it lands.
In Pulse — "One frame, held long enough to become a movie." The pulse is permission to hold.
The cut the jump
The jump between frames. All cut and no continuity is disorientation.
In Pulse — the days that blur are a strip of hard cuts with nothing held between them.
The seam the hinge
Where illustration becomes photograph — the inflection point.
In Pulse — the ring is the seam. Cold + fast painterly memory resolves into warm + slow lived photograph.
Grain & warmth the texture
The feel of film stock. Warm and slow reads as alive; cold and clean reads as data.
In Pulse — the whole style move is cold + fast → warm + slow. Grain over gloss, always.

Frames in the aperture — expanding & focusing the lens on the scene

Attention is an aperture. On autopilot it's stopped down and racing: a thousand frames flicker past the sensor and not one is exposed long enough to register. The week goes by unwitnessed — not empty, just never developed.

The pulse is a hand on the aperture ring. It opens. Light floods in, the iris widens, and the lens pulls focus off the plane you were bracing for and onto the one you're actually standing in. The scene, which was always there, resolves. That is the entire mechanism rendered as cinematography: presence is not more footage — it's the aperture widening and the focus racking until a single frame is held long enough to become a memory. Every hybrid frame in this library stages that moment; every motion clip below obeys a rule about which side of the aperture it lives on.

The illustration footthe memory, painted — in 3D

Rule — composed camera on rails, and the 2D world must show its 3D space (Disney multiplane). This is illustration once it has settled — the remembered, the universal, the sync at the end. Cut the scene into depth planes (foreground, midground, background) and glide them past the lens at different speeds, the way Disney's multiplane camera did — so parallax opens in every direction and the flat artwork reveals real dimensional space. The characters stay alive and three-dimensional: a soft breath, a blink, hair and fabric drifting, a small gesture — always enough motion that they read as dimensional beings, not paper dolls. Composed and dreamlike, never handheld: memory doesn't wobble, but it does breathe in depth. This is not the churn's camera — see the churn foot below for the one place illustration is allowed to lose its composure.

The churn footthe scattered search — illustration, uncomposed

Rule — the camera has ADHD. Frantic, sweeping, seeking, never landing. Scoped to the cold churn beats only — the one place illustration is allowed to break its own composure. Big searching swings across the depth planes, a whip-push toward one face then a jerk toward another before either resolves, a held frame that stutters like stop-motion instead of gliding. It's still multiplane — the depth is still real — but the camera can't sit still in it, because autopilot can't. The instant a beat settles into a held, composed frame, we've left the churn and entered the memory (or the turn). Never use this camera on the sync or the field of light — those are illustration that has arrived, not illustration that's still searching.

The churn isn't only a phone in the frame. A person can be fully present in body and still gone — walking a familiar street, doing the dishes, replaying a conversation that already happened or rehearsing one that hasn't. Make it literal: a small, faint, translucent film-frame hovers near their head, its own private screening, dimmer and cooler than the real room around them — no phone anywhere in the shot. These solitary mind-movie beats get the opposite camera from the crowd churn: held, quiet, almost too still, because the restlessness lives inside the little ghost-screen, not in the lens. Two references: journey-images/film-22-mind-movie-commute.png (the argument replayed on a sidewalk) and journey-images/film-23-mind-movie-chores.png (the conversation rehearsed at a sink).

The transition footthe reel slowing

Rule — illustration resolves into photograph on a push or pull, timed to the pulse. This is the inflection: a slow push-in or pull-out along the lens axis — zooming toward or away from the seam — as the painted side resolves into the photographic side, rack focus from blur into sharp, cold and fast easing into warm and slow. Never a lateral pan or a searching sweep here — the churn's frantic camera has already stopped by the time we reach the seam. The tap is the hinge that slows the reel. Use it once and let it land — it's the whole story in one move, so never overuse it.

The photograph footthe present, handheld

Rule — full photographic video feels handheld on a cell phone. Organic micro-drift, a slight tilt, a breath of zoom — imperfect on purpose. It's the intimacy of a real hand holding a real moment: warm, alive, present, un-staged. Never the locked, gliding, composed camera of the illustration foot — a lived moment has a pulse in the frame itself.

When to use whichlet the mood be intentional

Not a mechanical choice — a mood choice. Pick the foot by the feeling the moment should carry, and never mix them inside one shot except at a deliberate transition.

Illustration multiplane
When the mood is remembered, dreamlike, tender, universal — a feeling about life.
Because — it reads as memory and metaphor; the multiplane depth keeps it alive without pretending to be real.
The churn frantic multiplane
When the mood is scattered, seeking, autopilot itself — before there's anything to remember yet.
Because — the camera's composure IS the tell; take it away and the viewer feels the churn before a word is said.
Photograph handheld
When the mood is immediate, intimate, happening now, embodied — a feeling you're inside of.
Because — handheld realism puts the viewer in the room; the moment has a pulse.
50 / 50 transition the hinge
When the mood is the turn itself — autopilot becoming presence, the missed moment being caught.
Because — the medium change is the message. Earn it only at the hinge, never as decoration.

If a moment could be either, ask what you want the viewer to feel — remembered vs. lived — and let that decide. If the moment is still searching for itself, it's the churn; nothing has settled enough to be a memory yet.
